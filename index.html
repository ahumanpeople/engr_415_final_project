The Sense Hat

A 360° haptic obstacle detection system for visually impaired navigation, built into a wide-brimmed hat using Time-of-Flight sensors and an STM32 microcontroller.

Milton Tinoco · Kevin Huang · Jose Barrios
Explore the Project

Overview

Approximately 216 million people worldwide live with some form of visual impairment. Current navigation aids — most notably the white cane — provide only limited environmental awareness and require dedicated training. The Sense Hat addresses this gap by embedding a full 360° obstacle-detection system inside a discreet, everyday wide-brimmed hat.

When the user approaches an obstacle, vibration motors integrated into the hat's brim activate with an intensity that scales proportionally to proximity. The user receives spatial, directional feedback without any visual component — no screen, no audio, no device that draws attention in public.

Design Objectives

⚡

Reliability

Consistently detects nearby obstacles during indoor navigation and scales haptic intensity with distance, so the user always knows how close they are.

🎩

Discreet & Portable

All electronics are concealed inside the hat's crown and brim. The assembled device remains lightweight and suitable for all-day wear.

💰

Affordability

Designed with low-cost, off-the-shelf components. Total BOM cost comes in at approximately $157, making it accessible for real-world adoption.

Mechanical Design

The mechanical design is centered on a classic wide-brimmed black fedora, fully modeled in SolidWorks. The goal was to conceal every electronic component while keeping them accessible for maintenance and iteration.

Three VL53L5CX-SATEL Time-of-Flight sensors are mounted along the brim — two at the front for coverage of the user's primary field of motion and one at the rear — providing a near-360° sensing arc. Eight ERM haptic vibration motors are secured just above the brim's inner edge, each corresponding to a directional zone.

The STM32 microcontroller and LiPo battery are housed in a laser-cut basswood enclosure mounted on top of the hat's crown. Basswood was selected for its ease of laser-cutting at small scales and adequate thermal dissipation for outdoor use. 3D-printed PLA spacers create the necessary clearance to sandwich the battery and MCU board within the enclosure.

Electrical Design

The system is built around an STM32L476RG Nucleo board, selected for its faster ARM Cortex-M4 architecture over Arduino — necessary to simultaneously interpret 16+ data points from multiple ToF sensors and drive 8 independent haptic motor channels in real time.

Each VL53L5CX-SATEL sensor communicates over I²C, sharing the bus with address-select lines to differentiate between units. Motor control is handled via 8 GPIO pins configured for PWM output, each driving an S8050 NPN transistor that amplifies the MCU's logic-level current to the motor. A 1N4148 flyback diode in parallel with every motor protects against reverse-voltage spikes.

Power is supplied by a 3.7 V / 3000 mAh LiPo cell boosted to 5 V by an MT3608 DC-DC converter. FSR pressure sensors on the brim's inner edge trigger hardware interrupts to wake or sleep the system — a 1 kΩ pull-up resistor prevents signal jitter at each FSR node.

Software Design

The embedded firmware is structured as a finite state machine (FSM) that manages the full sensing-to-feedback pipeline. The system boots into a low-power idle state and activates only when the FSR sensors detect the hat being worn — reducing battery consumption during periods of non-use.

Embedded System Requirements

2× interrupt signals (FSR wear detection) I²C bus (4 ToF sensors) 8× PWM GPIO (haptic motors) UART (debug/PC comms) Low-power sleep modes 60 Hz sampling timer

Sensor Spatial Mapping

Each VL53L5CX-SATEL operates in a 3×3 multi-zone mode, returning 9 simultaneous distance measurements per sensor. With three sensors, the system collects up to 27 depth values per frame at up to 60 Hz.

A majority-voting filter is applied across 5–10 consecutive frames: for each spatial zone, the most frequently occurring distance value is selected as the representative reading. This eliminates noise and outlier measurements before the system decides whether to activate a motor — improving reliability and reducing false triggers.

Control Pseudocode

Interrupt — Start on Pressure Detected

LOOP  // Sensor collection
    New readings → Map[8×4]  (4 sensors × 8 zones)
    SAVE MAP[1], MAP[2], MAP[3], MAP[4]
    COMPARE MAPS  (majority voting)
    Final MAP READY
END LOOP

LOOP  // Motor control
    RESTART GPIO
    NearDistance[32] = 0
    NearLocation[32] = 0

    FOR each zone i in Map:
        IF Map[i] < threshold (e.g. 3 m):
            NearDistance[i] = Map[i]
            NearLocation[i] = i

    SWITCH NearLocation:
        CASE 1–8:
            Set PWM duty cycle ∝ NearDistance strength
            WAIT 50 ms
            RESTART GPIO
END LOOP

Interrupt — Stop on No Pressure Detected

At the end of each control cycle, all GPIO outputs are reset to logic-low as a safety measure. A watchdog timer resets the system if any loop stalls, ensuring the device never locks up in an uncontrolled state.

Results & Evaluation

Performance evaluation is grounded in the original design objectives. The primary test consists of a controlled navigation experiment: a participant wearing the Sense Hat attempts to move from a start point to a destination with eyes closed, relying solely on haptic feedback. Observations are recorded across multiple trials in different environments with varying obstacle configurations.

Evaluation Criteria

👁️

Discreteness

Visual inspection of the final product confirms that no electronic components are visible from the outside. The hat appearance is consistent with a standard wide-brimmed fedora.

🎯

Navigation Reliability

Participants are assessed on whether they can correctly interpret haptic direction and intensity cues to reach a destination and avoid obstacles without sight.

⚖️

Wearability

The assembled hat is evaluated for comfort, weight distribution, and ease of donning and doffing — including automatic on/off via FSR pressure sensing.

Known Challenges

Moisture & Sweat

Electronic components must be insulated from direct contact with the user's skin. The laser-cut housing provides a physical barrier while allowing passive heat dissipation from the MCU and battery.

Hat Orientation

Directional sensing accuracy depends on the hat remaining approximately level. Tilting or rotating the hat could cause false-positive detections in unintended zones. User awareness of proper positioning is the primary mitigation strategy.

Power Management

The STM32 is configured for low-power sleep whenever the hat is removed. Active run time is approximately 2.3 hours at full load; upgrading to a 6000 mAh cell doubles this to ~4.6 hours.
